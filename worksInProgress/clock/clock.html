Clock

Inspired by large mechanical clocks such as The Rathaus-Glockenspiel in Munich, which tells two stories, the Anker clock in Vienna, featuring a parade of historical figures while playing music of their era, and modern interpretations such as those by Tim Hunkins, for this project I will build an electro-mechanical clock out of locally sourced salvaged materials and mechanisms. At the end of the residency the clock will remain, as a work of art, as a timekeeper, and as a celebration of mechanisms.

These mechanisms would not be part of the time keeping system; rather, they perform gratuitous mechanical activity: moderate activity on the quarter-hour, more on the half-hour, and all-out on the hour. The various activities would be on a theme which will be determined when inspired by the site and gathered materials.

I have long been fascinated by mechanisms and the ingenuity of converting one type of motion to another. In the 21st century it is increasingly rare to see mechanisms as they are either replaced with electronic counterparts or hidden. I have observed that my students (and indeed the general public) seem to accept this "black box" either as something unimportant that is undeserving of attention (and perhaps even shameful to consider) or as something so complex as to be beyond the grasp of all but the most elite and nearly magical. Indeed, Frank Popper wrote “The introduction, suggestion and employment of machine movements in art, can be equated with the idea of the identiﬁcation with the industrial and mechanical universe that is as much within the ﬁeld of magic as that of aesthetics” (1968, 235). One goal of this clock is to draw attention to and celebrate these mechanisms.

Using salvaged materials critiques capitalist consumerism, which encourages discarding and replacing broken, old, or out of style items. I identify with a growing movement demanding that products be designed for maintenance, repair, and a long life.
